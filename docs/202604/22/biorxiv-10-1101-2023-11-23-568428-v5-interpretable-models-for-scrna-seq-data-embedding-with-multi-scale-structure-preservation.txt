# SUMMARY 16 

The ability to explore high-dimensional single-cell transcriptomics data efficiently is crucial in 17 

many biological studies. Dimensionality reduction techniques have therefore emerged as a basic 18 

building block of analytical workflows. They generate low-dimensional embeddings that capture 19 

important structures in the data, and are often used in discovery, quality control, and downstream 20 

analysis. However, the trustworthiness of current methods and the rigour of popular evaluation 21 

criteria are limited. We tackle this in an empirical study of structure-preserving data embeddings, 22 

delivering two tools. First, we introduce ViScore : a robust scoring framework that improves 23 

both unsupervised and supervised quality metrics, with emphasis on scalability and fairness. 24 

Second, we introduce ViVAE : a deep learning model that achieves better multi-scale structure 25 

preservation and is equipped with new tools for interpretability. We demonstrate the potential 26 

of these contributions to advance the trustworthiness of single-cell dimensionality reduction in a 27 

quantitative comparison and focused case studies. 28

# INTRODUCTION 31 

Data embedding using dimensionality reduction (DR) is common in the computational analysis of 32 

single-cell RNA-sequencing (scRNA-seq) data 1,2 . DR converts high-dimensional (HD) gene ex- 33 

pression data to low-dimensional (LD) embeddings (Figure 1 A). These embeddings are suitable 34 

for describing cell type diversity 2, quality control (QC) after data integration 3, or representing ten- 35 

tative trajectory structures 4. They generally improve the interpretability of single-cell datasets, 36 

which are sparse and noise-prone 5. DR is therefore crucial for effective exploratory data analysis 37 

in the single-cell bioinformatics domain. 38 

Current methods differ considerably in the criteria they optimise. Principal component analy- 39 

sis (PCA) is a popular deterministic method that obtains ordered orthogonal vectors explaining 40

applied to preprocess HD sparse data, including scRNA-seq 6. It is typically used to generate 50- 43 

to 100-dimensional representations amenable to downstream processing, including further DR. 44 

Alternatively, the top 2 or 3 PCs can be taken directly as the embedding. However, this inherently 45 

limits the ability to capture complex non-linear relationships between features, as PCA only uses 46 

linear transformations. 47 

Non-linear DR methods overcome this, with t-SNE 7 and UMAP 8 having gained special promi- 48 

nence in single-cell biology 2,9 . They both fall into the family of neighbour embedding (NE) al- 49 

gorithms, and can be formulated as optimising point positions via attractive and/or repulsive 50 

forces 10 . NE algorithms aim to preserve local point neighbourhoods, and are suitable for visu- 51 

alising clusters and comparing alternative labellings. However, their inherent ‘locality bias’ can 52 

distort the relative positions of cohesive cell clusters, introducing artificial gaps and arbitrary 53 

global layouts 11 . By locality bias , we mean that local structures in the data (bounded by some 54 

‘scale’ parameter that governs size of the point neighbourhoods of interest), are optimised (see 55 

Figure 1 B). While scale can be increased ( e.g. , the perplexity parameter in t-SNE or neighbour- 56 

hood count in UMAP ), the limitation to local structures remains. Some initialisation strategies 57 

partially compensate for this, yielding better preservation of large-scale structures 2. 58 

Current single-cell bioinformatics literature lacks formal definitions of the terms ‘local’ and 59 

‘global’ that would lend themselves clearly to extensive comparative evaluation across many 60 

datasets. There has been an emphasis on evaluation metrics that are straightforward to use 61 

in benchmarks involving representative data 12–14 . This has been in lieu of extensive descrip- 62 

tions of the underlying data manifolds, their intrinsic dimensionality, and dataset-specific topolo- 63 

gies, which is infeasible with much of single-cell omics. The present study, similarly, focuses on 64 

broadly applicable strategies that make few assumptions about the dataset in hand. 65 

Our work here, while focused on application more than on theory, offers a conceptual frame- 66 

work for the local/global dichotomy. By ‘local structures’ we refer to structures in some close 67 

neighbourhood of any given vantage point in the input data, with neighbourhood size governed 68 

by the scale parameter described above. Conversely, global structures are not limited by a 69 

scale parameter (Figure 1 C) and can extend further across the point cloud under this conceptual 70 

framework. The term ‘structure preservation’ (SP) is then used to indicate a measure of how well 71 

the original HD structures are preserved in the LD embeddings (despite inherent constraints due 72 

to reduced dimensionality). 73 

A number of recent methods have set out to increase the preservation of global structures, or 74 

to find a favourable local-global balance. Some, including TriMap 15 and PaCMAP 16 , are variants 75 

of NE. While NE methods are typically susceptible to partitioning data points into artificial ‘islands’ 76 

(Figure 1 D), both TriMap and PaCMAP sample some points outside of local neighbourhoods in 77 

the optimisation process to improve the global layout of embeddings. 78 

SQuad-MDS 17 is a recent adaptation of multidimensional scaling (MDS), which preserves 79 

relative HD pairwise distances between some points regardless of neighbourhood or structure 80 

scales. In practice, this optimises the global layout of embeddings, similarly to PCA despite 81 

algorithmic differences (Figure 1 E). 82 

Models such as scvis 18 and ivis 12 are examples of variational autoencoders (VAEs; 19 ), a 83 

flexible neural network architecture. VAEs embed HD data in a smooth latent space, favourable 84 

for representing continuous transitions between cell states (Figure 1 F). 85 

Overall, the trend of attempting to better balance local and global SP can reduce misinter- 86 

pretations of t-SNE and UMAP , which are often applied to data with important global or hierar- 87 

chical structures, despite not being optimised to represent them faithfully 20,21 . However, a major 88 

open problem remains. This is the issue of systematically evaluating the accuracy of embed- 89 

dings. Recent comparisons apply both unsupervised and supervised scores 13,14,22 , often via 90

These approaches exhibit some shortcomings. First, when scoring local structure preservation 92

More broadly, a recent critique 24 questioned the usefulness of single-cell DR due to failures to 98 

preserve distances between points. A response to it 25 demonstrated that t-SNE and UMAP pre- 99

a need for clear and fair evaluation criteria and indicators of embedding trustworthiness. 101 

Our ViScore framework addresses the concerns regarding evaluation and builds on the con- 102 

ceptual ‘local-versus-global’ distinction we drew above. ViScore introduces highly scalable un- 103 

supervised SP scoring with few assumptions on the data and embeddings (novel RNX curve ap- 104 

proximations), an improved qualitative supervised evaluation method ( Extended Neighbourhood- 105 

Proportion-Error ; xNPE), and a fully-fledged benchmarking framework, all of which we describe 106 

in this paper. 107 

With an empirical evaluation framework in place, we set out to develop a DR model for rep- 108 

resenting both local and global structures faithfully. ViVAE , our flexible deep learning-based 109 

method, exhibits a favourable balance between the two criteria. Furthermore, it incorporates 110 

encoder indicatrices (EIs): a tool for visualising unwanted distortions in existing embeddings. 111 

Grounded in previous work within differential geometry 26 , EIs serve as a straightforward QC 112 

check that is model- and dataset-specific and allows the investigator to pinpoint specific parts 113 

of the embedding that are artificially expanded, shrunk, or stretched. The aim of EIs is to boost 114 

the adoption of quality-control and explainability measures in DR, which has been limited so far. 115 

They are a flexible approach that represents as much information about embedding distortions 116 

in a 2-dimensional projections as possible, while remaining easy to interpret. 117

Concepts of ‘local’ and ‘global’ across disciplines and applications 127 

The notions of local and global scale are highly pertinent to the presented work. We note that understanding of these terms differs across disciplines of study. 

> 128 129

In topology and graph theory, ‘local’ and ‘global’ are complementary scales of description: the local focuses on what happens in a small neighbourhood, while the global asks how those parts fit into an entire system. In topology, the distinction is central to the difference between neighbourhood-level geometric information and whole-space invariants such as connected components or holes 28 . In graph theory, it appears in the contrast between measures based on a node’s immediate neighbours, such as degree ( i.e. , number of connections), and measures that depend on paths or structure across the entire graph, such as centrality 29 .

> 130 131 132 133 134 135 136

In applied topology and topological data analysis (TDA), local methods are used to capture fine-scale geometric variation, noise structure, and neighbourhood connectivity in data. In graphs and networks, local structure usually means the direct neighbourhood of a node: adjacent node counts, how tightly those neighbours connect, and how clustered the surrounding subgraph is. 

> 137 138 139 140 141

A global perspective, on the other hand, asks about properties of the entire space that cannot be recovered from one neighbourhood alone. In topology, classic invariants such as connected components and loops are global because they describe the overall shape of the object rather than one local region. In graphs and networks, global measures depend on relationships spanning much or all of the graph, so they reflect a node’s position in network-wide flow, connectivity, or integration. Graph-theoretic work on local-global convergence makes this distinction precise by separating descriptions that depend only on bounded neighbourhoods from those that incorporate larger-scale organization 30 .

> 142 143 144 145 146 147 148 149

In single-cell bioinformatics, ‘local’ and ‘global’ are used in multiple contexts. Some previous studies have applied notions from topological data analysis to describe local and global scales in the context of gene expression data 31,32 . However, within the subfield of dimensionality reduction (DR) and visualisation, the distinction in scale is often used in a highly operational way, with less focus on theoretical underpinnings. Here, local structure broadly refers to preserving relative neighbourhoods of similar cells, whereas global structure means preserving broader relationships among more distant cell populations, such as developmental branches, trajectories, or inter-cluster separation 13,14 .

> 150 151 152 153 154 155 156 157

The terms usually refer to the faithfulnesss of an embedding or manifold representation with respect to the original input data, not directly to topological invariants or network metrics. By contrast, in topology the local/global distinction is about the mathematical scale at which a property is defined, and in graph theory it is about whether a measure depends on immediate neighbourhoods or on the graph as a whole.

# RESULTS 164 

First, we present a framework for evaluating DR in both supervised and unsupervised settings 165 

(with and without the use of cell labels, respectively). This is implemented in the ViScore Python 166 

package, which includes an automated large-scale benchmarking solution. 167 

Figure 2 A introduces RNX curves 33 as the basis for assessing structure preservation (SP). 168 

This is a mathematical framework for quantifying preservation of structure at any given scale. 169 

Crucially, we solve the outstanding issue of poor scalability of RNX with a custom approximation 170 

algorithm that extends their use to large scRNA-seq data. This has been unfeasible previously, 171 

thus dramatically restricting the evaluation of single-cell DR. We use RNX to derive two metrics 172 

which score, respectively, local (locally biased) and global (scale-agnostic) SP, and use them to 173 

compare ViVAE to other methods. 174 

Figure 2 B (left side) shows the Extended Neighbourhood-Proportion-Error (xNPE): a pro- 175 

posed score for supervised (cell label-based) evaluation. xNPE, based on the NPE 22 , measures 176 

embedding quality per cell population. It captures distortions that change the relative positions 177 

between labelled cell populations. Providing cell population-level scores instead of embedding- 178 

level ones, xNPE serves as a QC check especially for comparing multiple candidate embeddings 179 

of the same dataset. 180 

Another QC tool shown in Figure 2 B (right side) are neighbourhood composition plots (NCPs), 181 

which diagnose sources of mis-embedding. They provide a qualitative description of errors, in 182 

contrast to the quantitative approach of the xNPE. NCPs visualise, for a given population of inter- 183 

est, which cells surround it, respectively, in the HD input space and in the evaluated embedding. 184 

This is done using a range of local neighbourhood scales. 185 

Second, with a suitable evaluation framework in place we developed ViVAE : a DR model 186 

for balanced local and global structure preservation. This is a regularised variational autoen- 187 

coder (VAE) that learns non-linear transformations of scRNA-seq data (Figure 2 C). The input 188 

data is first denoised to prevent the model fitting to noise. Next, the model is trained on the 189 

denoised data with the use of a proposed stochastic multidimensional scaling ( stochastic-MDS ) 190 

regulariser. Stochastic-MDS is a fundamental extension of the ‘stochastic quartet’ approach 34 , 191 

making adaptations to yield a differentiable loss function that is usable in a deep learning con- 192 

text. In practice, stochastic-MDS improves the preservation of global structures and embedding 193 

distortions (as shown further). 194 

The trained model can generate data embeddings (Figure 2 D) and map previously unseen 195 

data points from the same domain as training data, facilitating the assessment of technical arti- 196 

facts by comparing data batches. 197 

Figure 2 E introduces encoder indicatrices (EIs): a QC tool that allows the investigator to vi- 198 

sually inspect unwanted distortions which the model introduces into the embedding. Appearing 199 

as ellipses in the figure, EIs denote both the direction and the magnitude of unwanted expan- 200 

sion, shrinkage, or stretching of parts of the embedding space, to the extent that these can be 201 

visualised in 2-d. 202 

Additionally, ViVAE integrates with FlowSOM 35 , which couples clustering and graph embed- 203 

ding via minimum spanning trees (MSTs), as shown in Figure 2 F (left side). This helps in validat- 204 

ing the MST topology against the embedding, and vice versa, and examining whether expected 205 

ontologies, hierarchies, or developmental lineages are reflected. Projecting values from the input 206 

feature space onto representative clusters also quickly captures major sources of variability, as 207 

a dataset-specific explanatory mechanism (right side). 208 

Both ViScore and ViVAE are available in dedicated online repositories, with several guided 209 

tutorials, experiments, and benchmarking guidelines provided alongside the software itself. 210

ViScore evaluates structure preservation across multiple scales 211 

The proposed ViScore methodology assesses the preservation of high-dimensional (HD) struc- 212 

tures in their low-dimensional (LD) embeddings across different scales. The term ‘Structure 213 

Preservation’ (SP) is taken to denote the degree to which an embedding retains the neigh- 214 

bourhood structures between HD points in the original data. The choice to focus on Euclidean 215 

distance-induced neighbourhoods, rather than distances themselves, is motivated by a previous 216 

analysis demonstrating their conceptual relevance within single-cell DR 25 . 217 

Figure 3 A shows an example of 4 alternative embeddings of a developmental dataset 4. A sin- 218 

gle ‘vantage-point’ cell is chosen (orange cross); a colour gradient shows the true neighbourhood- 219 

ranks of all other cells with respect to the vantage point. These neighbourhood-ranks are com- 220 

puted from the HD input data, not the embeddings. 221 

t-SNE and UMAP separate the data into islands, achieving a high level of detail (note that 222 

these separations always need to be validated as non-artificial). On the other hand, the gradi- 223 

ent of neighbour ranks is inconsistent: this demonstrates failure to preserve some structures. 224 

SQuad-MDS , at the other extreme, shows minimal separation into islands, but the gradient is 225 

preserved better. Finally, ViVAE balances these two criteria (as examined further in the following 226 

section). 227 

Despite under-defined notions of ‘local’ and ‘global’ in relevant literature, we devise exact def- 228 

initions for a Global SP score and Local SP score, both based on RNX curves 33 (see Methods ). 229 

This is shown in Figure 3 B. It is crucial that we avoid setting a hard neighbourhood-size cut-off 230 

for what constitutes a ‘local‘ structure, and we compute Global SP as a truly scale-agnostic score 231 

(i.e. , small-scale and large-scale structures are treated as equally important), thereby reducing 232 

arbitrary designs. 233 

In overview, both SP scores measure the preservation across all possible scales; for any 234 

point in a dataset of size N , these are scales from 1 to N − 1. While Global SP treats all 235 

scales as equally important for the final score ( scale-agnostic ), Local SP applies a log 10 rescaling 236 

of neighbourhood scales, which upweights the importance of smaller-scale structures ( locally 237 

biased , as proposed in an earlier study 33 ). 238 

The SP scores are upper-bounded by 1, with 0 corresponding to a random embedding (base- 239 

line), and negative values to a worse-than-random representation of neighbourhood structures. 240 

A realistic upper bound, however, is dataset-specific, since full preservation of neighbourhoods 241 

would only be possible for some cases of noiseless data lying on a 2-manifold that can be flat- 242 

tened. Determining this upper bound would translate to describing the true manifold of each 243 

single-cell dataset; this is not currently feasible. However, based on comparative analyses pre- 244 

sented here and elsewhere 13,14 , we know some DR methods to effectively prioritise Global SP 245 

(PCA, SQuad-MDS ) while others (mainly t-SNE) focus on Local SP. This knowledge serves as 246 

an empirically determined anchor for good performance with respect to each criterion. 247

faithfully, there are other notable aspects of ViVAE . Based on a VAE architecture, ViVAE is a 313

retain more continuity, despite the low cell counts. 354 

This raises the question of whether smooth embeddings represent transitional states cor- 355 

rectly. Consider that this data is longitudinal, still allowing for gaps in trajectories between hpf 356 

stages. To test this, we quantify how continuous or fragmented each lineage is in the input (HD) 357 

space. In Figure 4 C, we show Euclidean distances between cells ordered by pseudotime (‘tran- 358 

sitions’) within the prechordal plate lineage. This is computed for input space and for each of 359 

the embeddings, and transformed to account for scale differences (via min-max and log1p scal- 360 

ing). Large distance values at the 3.3- and 3.8-hpf stages in input space validate that the less 361 

dense representation of these cell subsets by smooth embeddings is correct; t-SNE and UMAP 362 

collapse them artificially. 363 

To measure the distortion of these transitions by each embedding, we report L1 distances 364 

between the input and embedded distance distributions, normalised by each lineage’s total cell 365 

count. These values (Figure 4 D) show that the smooth DR models indeed achieve a more faithful 366 

representation of the lineages. Furthermore, Figure 4 E shows transition distances in the ELC 367 

lineage to validate the bifurcation observed in embeddings. The real separation is shown to be 368 

gradual, more similar to the representation by the smooth embeddings. 369 

Encoder indicatrices reveal distortions in VAE model space 370 

We propose encoder indicatrices (EIs): a tool to visualise distortions of (V)AE-based latent 371 

spaces, and use them to analyse VAE, default ViVAE , and ViVAE-EncoderOnly embeddings 372 

of the Farrell data. Figure 5 A gives an overview of the algorithm. In each case, an embedding is 373 

generated by a trained encoder model. Representative points are sampled from the embedding 374 

using a uniform grid. They are then mapped to their coordinates in the input (HD) space. For 375 

each sampled point, a small hypersphere around it forms its HD indicatrix . Here, ‘indicatrix’ de- 376 

notes a shape that helps us describe how the particular part of the point cloud gets projected by 377 

the trained model. Using a local linearisation of the encoding, we then flatten the indicatrix and 378 

transform it using the encoder again. 379 

This transformation introduces size and shape distortions, which get mapped onto the em- 380 

bedding coordinates. In this way, we objectively describe local embedding artifacts throughout 381 

the latent space, to the extent that this is achievable in a 2-dimensional visualisation. In line 382 

with previous related work 26 , we adopt the use of ellipses superimposed on the embedding for a 383 

readily interpretable visual description of artifacts. 384 

Figure 5 B uses EIs to highlight commonly observed embedding distortions in VAEs. First, 385 

scale inconsistences make some parts of the latent space artificially sparser. Second, a ‘run- 386 

away’ effect causes directed stretching of some point subsets. Third, some sparse regions are 387 

ill-defined, showing drastic extension where the embedding should be smoother. 388 

Figure 5 C shows that the default ViVAE model, with denoising and stochastic-MDS regular- 389 

isation, gives more uniform indicatrices, reducing scale inconsistencies and run-away effects. 390 

Some instabilities in sparse regions as seen with VAEs remain. Figure 5 D shows that the 391

Here we demonstrate the usefulness of ViVAE as a general-purpose DR algorithm for other than 397

Specifically, we use the Reed immune cell dataset, originating from a single-cell transcrip- 402 

tomic atlas of the adult human breast 27 . As opposed to the Farrell embryological data (in pre- 403

with distinct populations of mature immune cells, likely presenting a challenge to our smoother 405 

VAE-based embedding method. 406 

Figure 6 A compares ViVAE to the prevalent NE methods ( t-SNE and UMAP ), showing a de- 407 

gree of major cell-compartment separation in each. Since DR invariably introduces artifacts, we 408

level SP scoring. Figure 6 B quantifies population-level embedding errors using xNPE, which 410 

leverages the provided cell labelling. For each population of interest (POI), the xNPE aggre- 411 

gates the distribution of ‘self versus non-self’ neighbours (how many neighbouring cells belong 412 

to the POI itself, versus a different population) over all the cells in the POI. It does so separately 413 

in the HD input space and each LD embedding, ending up with two distributions. These two 414 

distributions are then compared using the earth mover’s distance (EMD). The lower the EMD 415 

value, the lower the distribution of the POI is with respect to the ground truth. 416 

By this metric, t-SNE fares less well than UMAP for 8 of 14 populations and less well than 417 

ViVAE for 12 of 14 populations, despite a high Local SP score based in an unsupervised setting. 418 

Importantly, in contrast to Local SP, xNPE only focuses on how each population interfaces with 419 

the others around it; small positional inaccuracies or swapping positions within the population are 420 

not penalised. (The online ViScore repository includes a dedicated proof-of-concept experiment 421 

with synthetic data.) 422

# DISCUSSION 447 

We have shown ViVAE to achieve favourable multi-scale structure preservation and faithful em- 448 

bedding of both trajectory and non-trajectory data, according to comparisons with other methods 449 

on an array of real biological datasets as well as focused case studies. Integrating denoising of 450 

inputs and a novel stochastic-MDS component with a VAE model, ViVAE balances the continu- 451 

ous nature of VAE latent spaces with an ability to separate major cell compartments. To improve 452 

interpretability, we equip ViVAE with QC functionality in the form of encoder indicatrices ; cru- 453 

cially, these can be implemented for any differentiable DR model. In addition to a regularised 454 

VAE, we propose and evaluate a decoder-less model ( ‘ViVAE-EncoderOnly’ ). We take the full 455 

model as default due to mostly slightly better preservation of local structures, but evaluate both. 456 

We have demonstrated that balancing local and global structure preservation can lead to 457 

more faithful embeddings of developmental, longitudinal, but also non-trajectory (single-snapshot) 458 

data. In essence, the proposed methodology is designed for a broad range of exploratory data 459 

analysis workflows. 460 

Throughout the work, we use Euclidean distances in PCA-reduced input space, which yield 461 

evaluations that are generally congruent with the known trade-offs between local and global 462 

structure preservation by established methods. However, for adaptability to sparser and higher- 463 

dimensional data ( e.g. , expression of a filtered gene set, without initial PCA reduction), we im- 464 

plement the use of cosine distances as an alternative for use with the stochastic-MDS loss, as 465 

well as in the unsupervised structure-preservation scoring. 466 

The development of ViVAE followed our initial implementation of model-agnostic structure 467 

preservation scores in ViScore , which were necessary for a quantitative evaluation. Overcom- 468 

ing previous conceptual and computational limitations, we coin unsupervised embedding-level 469 

scores that quantify structure preservation without using hard local/global cut-offs, landmark 470 

points based on clustering, or other previously proposed proxy measures. In addition, we intro- 471 

duce supervised population-level evaluation metrics for comparing embeddings and explaining 472 

artifacts. 473 

Our contributions to evaluation and interpretability are motivated by increasing sizes of single- 474 

cell datasets, the availability of large public databases and atlases 42–44 , and ongoing efforts to 475 

systematically evaluate single-cell data analysis algorithms 11 . DR currently aids in exploration 476 

and hypothesis formulation, and its usage will likely rise with more public data. This will require 477 

efforts to prevent erroneous conclusions from spurious patterns or artifacts in embeddings. 478 

Having adapted notions from differential geometry 26 to visualise distortions in the model 479 

space using encoder indicatrices , we estimate that an increased use of such explainability tools 480 

will help in describing method-specific artifacts. Future endeavours in interpretable non-linear 481 

single-cell DR may involve hierarchical embeddings 45 and interactive tools for evaluating em- 482 

bedding quality at local levels 46,47 . 483

Limitations of this study 484 

Another major avenue of research involves topological data analysis (TDA) to describe point 485 

clouds. We acknowledge that concepts such as local and global structure have undergone thor- 486 

ough and formal examination within this field 28 . Further, some studies have sought to tackle the 487 

problem of interesting structures existing at multiple scales in single-cell data 48,49 . Nonetheless, 488 

this work within TDA has not been fully incorporated within the single-cell DR field. While inter- 489 

esting work on topology-preserving autoencoders exists 50 , our familiarity with a recent study on 490 

topological regularisation of autoencoders for single-cell DR 51 emphasises to us that dataset- 491 

specific descriptions of topologies can prove cumbersome and non-trivial. 492 

More broadly, describing the true manifolds of real biological data is currently intractable. 493 

Therefore, the focus of our study here, and a handful of others that compare DR methodolo- 494 

gies 12–14,25 , is instead on empirical evaluation across different datasets that are representative 495 

of a range of real-world scenarios. 496 

In line with this approach, our work seeks to provide ways to measure and optimise multi- 497 

scale structure preservation without requiring too much tuning or too many assumptions on un- 498 

derlying structures. This is why our current study does not use TDA and remains restricted to 499 

the use of nearest-neighbour graphs in lieu of more elaborate topologies. 500 

Nonetheless, our ongoing research is focusing on alternative graph structures for both de- 501 

noising and DR evaluation, such as networks from the Louvain algorithm 52 , witness complexes 49 , 502 

or branched spanning trees robust to noise 53 . Our integration of ViVAE with FlowSOM 54 (shown 503 

in Figure 1) is a tentative step toward the use of graphs together with embedded point clouds to 504 

capture relevant structures. 505

Encoder indicatrices for detection of local distortion 984 

During training, AEs and VAEs both learn a smooth latent space, defined by the encoder. The 985 

parametric nature of these models, along with the fact that the transformations they learn are 986 

differentiable, allow us to explore properties of this latent space. Specifically, we can detect local 987 

contraction, expansion and directed stretching of its different parts. 988 

Our methodology extends the work on geometric regularisation for (non-variational) AEs 26 . 989 

To visualise undesirable artifacts, the use of indicatrices 60 is proposed to quantify latent space 990 

distortion. An indicatrix can be understood, in a simplified way, as a circle on the surface of a 991 

small hypersphere in the original (HD) ambient space, embedded into the (LD) latent space. In a 992 

previously published study 26 , multiple indicatrices from different parts of the HD point cloud are 993 

sampled and then pulled back into the latent space through the decoder, to reveal distortions 994 

introduced by the model. 995 

A perfect, non-distorted embedding results in all indicatrices remaining circular and identical 996 

(circular because shape is preserved and identical because there are no scale inconsistencies 997 

across different parts of the latent space). The distortion that each indicatrix reveals is specific 998 

with respect to the ambient space, transformation function and the coordinates of the sampled 999 

point. In the previous study we reference 26 , indicatrices are computed for the transformation 1000 

learned by the decoder: we can refer to them therefore as ‘decoder indicatrices’. Crucially, 1001 

decoder indicatrices quantify distortion of latent space specifically with respect to the decoded 1002 

reconstruction ˆX. 1003 

In contrast, we derive a solution to the ‘encoder indicatrix’ problem, i.e. , quantifying local em- 1004 

bedding (and latent space) distortion with respect to the original high-dimensional inputs. Since 1005 

a general analytical solution is not feasible (essentially due to the encoder not being injective), 1006 

we provide a numerical solution, described in Note S2 . A schematic overview of the algorithm is 1007 

given in Figure 5 A. 1008

Rank-based neighbourhood preservation scoring 1032 

We base our unsupervised quality-assessment measures on widely adopted rank-based DR 1033 

evaluation criteria 61 . The basis for these is the concept of a co-ranking matrix , which quanti- 1034 

fies the changes of ranks of neighbours to each point in terms of pairwise distances in LD, as 1035 

compared to HD. 1036 

For any pair of HD points, we denote distance from ϵi to ϵj as δij . Conversely, we denote 1037 

the distance from an LD point xi to xj as dij . We define the rank in HD of ϵj with respect to ϵi 1038 

as the set cardinality ρij = |{ k : δik < δ ij ∨ (δik = δij ∧ k < j )}| . (This can be re-stated more 1039 

simply as ‘ ϵj is the ρij -th neighbour to ϵi’.) The corresponding rank in LD is similarly defined as 1040 

rij = |{ k : dik < d ij ∨ (dik = dij ∧ k < j )}| . The non-reflexive (non-self) neighbourhoods of ϵi 1041 

and xi are then written as νKi = {j : 1 ≤ ρij ≤ K} and vKi = {j : 1 ≤ rij ≤ K} respectively, 1042 

for a neighbourhood size of K. We then define the co-ranking matrix for a dataset of size N as 1043 

Q = [ qkl ]1≤k,l ≤N −1, where qkl = |{ (i, j ) : ρij = k ∧ rij = l}| . Any off-diagonal positive values in 1044 

this matrix indicate a change of rank in the LD embedding versus the HD input dataset, and are 1045 

undesirable. The co-ranking matrix records changes in the order of relative distances between 1046 

points as we go from the original, high-dimensional data, toward a lower-dimensional embedding. 1047 

A reduction in rank is labelled an intrusion and is recorded left of the diagonal, whereas an 1048 

increase is termed an extrusion , and is recorded right of the diagonal. A change in rank that 1049

neighbourhood of its reference) is termed a hard-K intrusion or hard-K extrusion , respectively. 1051

Extended Neighbourhood-Proportion-Error 1146 

The Neighbourhood Proportion Error (NPE) 13,22 , quantifies preservation of the structure of man- 1147 

ually defined populations of cells. We coin the Extended Neighbourhood-Proportion-Error (xNPE) 1148 

as a modification of the NPE. 1149 

For each labelled population, NPE iterates over all the points it contains as vantage points. 1150 

For each vantage point, the number of points in its k-ary neighbourhood belonging to the same 1151 

population is counted. This is done for the HD input data, as well as the LD embeddings. The 1152 

distribution of counts of same-population neighbours per vantange point is computed, and it is 1153 

compared between HD and LD using total variation distance (TVD), resulting in a population- 1154 

wise error score. The sum of these population-wise error scores is then returned as a measure 1155 

of error for the entire embedding. 1156 

In xNPE, we use proportions of same-population neighbours rather than counts. Further, 1157 

we use Earth mover’s distance (EMD) 71,72 to compute differences between distributions, since it 1158 

uses the entire distribution instead of the (more likely spurious) single largest difference between 1159 

distributions, and return population-wise scores to avoid an assumption of consistent resolution 1160 

of the cell population labelling. 1161 

Additionally, we scale each population-wise score. This is first done such that a score of 0 1162 

(best) is perfect and 1 (worst) is the upper bound which represents largest possible EMD value. 1163 

By default, we then adjust the score again such that 1 corresponds to a random embedding 1164 

baseline (as described in the corresponding pseudocode in Note S3 ). 1165 

Rather than summing or averaging population-wise errors to obtain an embedding-wise score, 1166 

we inspect and compare the population errors to detect which parts of the input point cloud are 1167 

the most mis-embedded. A proof-of-concept with synthetic data that compares xNPE to the 1168 

unsupervised Local and Global SP scores is presented in the online ViScore repository. 1169
